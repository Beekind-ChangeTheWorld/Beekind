Bees At La Chataignerai!

May 23, 2023 | New! Project at Ecolint

This summer, the La Chatagneraie campus was buzzed to welcome its new pollinators, the bees! Through the dedication and cooperation of numerous generous individuals, we were delighted to place a beehive behind the Lausanne building. The natural environment surrounding the campus and the careful care from the BeeKind team has allowed the beehive to thrive and grow sufficiently to survive the winter.

What is a beehive?

A beehive is the home of bees and is where they spend the majority of their lives. Beehives are composed of numerous hive bodies, each serving a specific function such as storing pollen, honey, or brood. Depending on the season and location, a beehive’s population can range from 10,000–80,000 individuals. Within a beehive, there are three different kinds of bees: worker bees, drones and the queen, with worker bees making up roughly 98% of the total. Despite this overwhelming majority, all the bees play a crucial role in the survival and balance of the beehive.

Bees at La Châtaigneraie…How?

The project to have bees at la Chataigneraie began in the summer of 2023 when Henrik felt inspired to continue his passion for bees in Switzerland. Previously, Henrik had 4 hives in Sweden, where he gained 5 years of experience and expertise. Firstly, administrative work needed to be completed in order to ensure the project was viable and was going to be accepted by the school. Thereafter, we achieved a PTA grant, which gave us the money to kickstart the project and buy all the necessary material. Finally, we needed the bees, which we kindly bought off a Swiss beekeeper. Over the remaining summer, the bees grew tremendously and are now in a state of hibernation to survive the harsh winter.

Our project and goals:

Our project BeeKind, will have the main collective goals of raising awareness/educating about the importance of pollinators, introducing bees and beekeeping to the Ecolint community, increasing school biodiversity and producing local organic honey. Overall, these goals have the common aim of informing people about the plummeting world bee populations and the repercussions this might have on the natural world and on humans.
